Three Works in My Gallery

Shenzhe Zhu · May 9, 2026

The homepage gallery is not meant to be decoration. It is a small, changing shelf of artworks I keep returning to: a few images that slow the page down before the academic things begin. The first three works are all, in different ways, about looking carefully at ordinary forms until they become charged with weather, memory, or friendship.

Claude Monet, Stacks of Wheat (End of Summer), 1890-91

Monet's wheatstacks were painted near his home at Giverny across 1890 and 1891. The subject is almost stubbornly simple: stacks of harvested grain, seen again and again as the light changes. That repetition is the point. The stacks are not there to become more dramatic; they are there so that time, atmosphere, and color can become visible.

I love this work because it treats attention as a serious method. A field, a stack, a horizon: nothing grand is happening, and yet everything is happening in the surface. It is a good reminder that a small object can become inexhaustible if one keeps looking.

Claude Monet, Villas at Bordighera, 1884

In early 1884, Monet traveled to Bordighera on the Italian Riviera. The Mediterranean light and the dense, unfamiliar vegetation pushed him into a different register: brighter, more tangled, almost overheated. This painting was later made in his Giverny studio from a smaller work painted on site, and it was intended as a decorative panel for Berthe Morisot's drawing room.

I like how excessive it feels. The garden does not behave like a clean scenic view; it grows, overlaps, and presses forward. Compared with the wheatstacks, this is Monet in a more luxuriant key, still chasing light, but now through palms, villas, and the strange abundance of the south.

Xia Baoyuan, Portrait of Chen Danqing, 1976

This portrait belongs to a very different world. Xia Baoyuan and Chen Danqing were part of the Shanghai artist circle that mattered deeply to Chinese realist painting in the 1970s. Chen would later become widely known for the Tibetan Series and for his writing and public art criticism, but here he appears before that later reputation has hardened around him.

What I love is the intimacy of one artist looking at another. The portrait is quiet, direct, and psychologically present. In the gallery, it keeps the Monets from becoming only a story about landscape and light. It brings in the face, the studio, friendship, and the strange historical charge of being young before one knows what one will become.
